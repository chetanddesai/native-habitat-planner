# Example Yard Placement — Poway, CA

A worked example showing how to distribute the 25 plants in [`data/plants-poway-ca.json`](../data/plants-poway-ca.json) across a typical Poway lot with five distinct planting zones. Each plant is assigned to its **preferred** zone based on the documented `sunExposure` and `slopeRequirements` fields, with notes on alternate zones where the plant would also perform well.

This document is illustrative — it's meant to help readers translate the inventory into a real planting plan. Adapt to your own site by noting where each plant's preferred conditions overlap your yard's microclimates.

---

## The Yard — Five Zones

| # | Location              | Aspect & Topography                | Light            | Key Constraints                                              |
| - | --------------------- | ---------------------------------- | ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 1 | Front of the house    | East-facing **steep slope**        | Full sun         | Morning sun, afternoon shade; sharp drainage; erosion-prone  |
| 2 | South side            | South-facing **flat**              | Full sun         | Maximum heat exposure; no slope-drainage advantage           |
| 3 | North side            | South-facing **steep slope**       | Partial shade    | Shaded by the house; slope drainage; cooler microclimate     |
| 4 | Back yard (slope)     | South-facing **steep slope**       | Full sun         | Quintessential chaparral / coastal sage scrub exposure       |
| 5 | Back yard (under tree)| West-facing **flat**               | Dappled shade (2–4 hrs filtered sun under tree canopy) | Canyon-bottom analog; moister soil; tree-understory niche |

## Zone 1 — Front: East-facing steep slope, full sun

**Six plants.** Morning sun, afternoon respite, and sharp slope drainage create a classic east-facing scrub-edge habitat. Plants here favor banks, rocky terraces, and species that appreciate cooler afternoons inland.

### Red Bush Monkeyflower (*Diplacus puniceus*) — small shrub
Slope notes literally read *"rocky banks, **north- and east-facing scrub edges**, and raised planters with sharp drainage."* This is its strongest match on the property.
**Also fine in:** Zone 3 (light shade tolerant).

### California Buckwheat (*Eriogonum fasciculatum*) — small shrub, keystone
"Dry slopes, berms, and raised planters with sharp drainage" — happy on any sunny well-drained slope.
**Also fine in:** Zones 2, 4.

### Bush Sunflower (*Encelia californica*) — small shrub
Warm coastal and inland slopes; tolerates wind and salt spray. The east aspect provides slightly cooler afternoons than its preferred south-slope habitat, but the species is broadly adapted.
**Also fine in:** Zones 2, 4.

### California Fuchsia (*Epilobium canum*) — herbaceous perennial
Slope notes: *"rocky banks, wall tops, and berms with fast drainage; afternoon shade acceptable inland."* East-facing slope = morning sun + afternoon shade, an ideal match for an inland Poway garden.
**Also fine in:** Zones 3, 4.

### Showy Penstemon (*Penstemon spectabilis*) — herbaceous perennial
Sun exposure: *"Full Sun (afternoon light inland)."* East-facing slopes give this inland-tolerant penstemon the cooler PM it appreciates while keeping the bank drainage it needs.
**Also fine in:** Zones 3, 4.

### Caterpillar Scorpionweed (*Phacelia cicutaria*) — groundcover annual
Sandy, gravelly, or rocky slopes; openings in chaparral; tolerates partial shade. Self-sows along open slope edges.
**Also fine in:** Zones 2, 3, 4.

## Zone 2 — South side: South-facing flat, full sun

**Six plants.** Maximum sun and heat with no slope to help with drainage — this zone needs plants that handle exposed flats. Open meadow species, annual wildflowers, and a few heat-tolerant heat-radiating-roadside specialists work well here.

### Narrowleaf Milkweed (*Asclepias fascicularis*) — herbaceous perennial, keystone
Slope notes call out *"swales, meadow pockets, **and gentle slopes** where winter moisture lingers."* This is the milkweed that handles flat sites best — and as a monarch host plant supporting 286 wildlife species, it's the single highest-impact plant on the list.
**Also fine in:** Zone 1 (gentle slope).

### Arroyo Lupine (*Lupinus succulentus*) — groundcover annual, keystone
Slope notes: *"**sandy flats**, road cuts, and gentle slopes."* The only annual on the list explicitly adapted to flat ground.
**Also fine in:** Zone 1.

### California Poppy (*Eschscholzia californica*) — groundcover annual
"Open grassy places, sunny openings in chaparral, valley grassland" — flat sunny ground is its native habitat. Self-sows generously.
**Also fine in:** Zones 1, 4.

### Parry's Phacelia (*Phacelia parryi*) — groundcover annual
Open slopes in coastal sage scrub; recently burned or disturbed ground. Tolerates flat exposed sites with lean soil.
**Also fine in:** Zones 1, 4.

### Desert Broom (*Baccharis sarothroides*) — large shrub, keystone
"Dry banks, roadsides, and coarse alluvium; **tolerates reflected heat**." One of the few shrubs that handles a flat south-facing exposure without slope drainage.
**Also fine in:** Zones 1, 4.

### Short-winged Deerweed (*Acmispon glaber*) — groundcover perennial
"Disturbed cuts, road verges, and dry slopes where roots can run deep." Deep taproot lets it survive a hot exposed flat better than most groundcovers.
**Also fine in:** Zones 1, 4.

## Zone 3 — North side: South-facing steep slope, partial shade

**Four plants.** Partial shade is the limiting factor — this zone is for plants that explicitly tolerate or prefer cooler, more sheltered conditions. The slope provides drainage; the shade mimics a north-facing or canyon microclimate.

### Engelmann Oak (*Quercus engelmannii*) — large tree, keystone
Sun: *"Full Sun to Partial Shade."* Slope: *"deep soils on slopes and mesas."* A rare Poway-region endemic — this is its most natural placement on the lot. Supports 265 wildlife species.
**Also fine in:** Zones 1, 4 (will tolerate more sun once established).

### Coast Live Oak (*Quercus agrifolia*) — large tree, keystone
Sun: *"Full Sun to Partial Shade (**seedlings appreciate afternoon shade**)."* Slope: *"hillsides, ravine sides, and terrace sites with room for lateral roots."* Partial shade is ideal for establishment; this is the zone where it'll thrive without supplemental water past year 3. Supports 278 wildlife species — the highest in the inventory.
**Also fine in:** Zones 1, 4.

### Woollyleaf Ceanothus (*Ceanothus tomentosus*) — large shrub, keystone
Slope notes: *"Prefers slopes that retain slightly more moisture than average — **north-facing slopes, slope bottoms, or east/west-facing slopes** with heavier soils and rocks."* A partial-shade south slope mimics that cooler-moister microclimate. Caterpillar host to 83 butterfly/moth species.
**Also fine in:** Zone 1.

### Toyon (*Heteromeles arbutifolia*) — large shrub, keystone
Sun: *"Full Sun to **Light Shade**."* The classic oak-understory shrub of southern California chaparral. Red winter berries feed birds — Hollywood is literally named after this plant.
**Also fine in:** Zones 1, 4, 5 (genuinely flexible — could move to Zone 5 to bulk up the under-tree planting).

## Zone 4 — Back: South-facing steep slope, full sun

**Seven plants.** This is *the* zone — quintessential coastal sage scrub. Most of the small shrubs and aromatic perennials in the Poway inventory evolved for exactly this exposure: hot sun, steep slope, fast drainage, summer drought. Plant densely; these species support each other and minimize weed pressure.

### Laurel Sumac (*Malosma laurina*) — large shrub
Slope notes: *"**Warm south- and west-facing slopes**; tolerates wind exposure."* A signature SoCal chaparral shrub, often the dominant species on hot south-facing exposures.
**Also fine in:** Zones 1, 2.

### Mission Manzanita (*Xylococcus bicolor*) — large shrub, keystone
Slope notes: *"Slopes and hillsides on fast-draining soils; **excellent on south-facing chaparral exposures**."* San Diego County endemic — this is its native habitat with no irrigation. The genus *Arctostaphylos*/*Xylococcus* is a chaparral keystone.
**Also fine in:** Zone 1 (only if drainage is sharp).

### White Sage (*Salvia apiana*) — small shrub, keystone
"Raised berms, dry slopes, and coarse soils with excellent air drainage." Strictly intolerant of summer wet feet — needs a fast-draining slope. Iconic ceremonial sage of the region.
**Also fine in:** Zone 1.

### Black Sage (*Salvia mellifera*) — small shrub, keystone
"Dry slopes, rock gardens, and berms with air movement." Pairs naturally with White Sage; together they make the aromatic backbone of coastal sage scrub.
**Also fine in:** Zones 1, 2.

### California Sagebrush (*Artemisia californica*) — small shrub, keystone
"Dry slopes, coastal bluffs, and berms with continuous air movement." The third member of the coastal sage scrub trinity (with the two salvias above). Hosts coastal California gnatcatcher habitat.
**Also fine in:** Zones 1, 2.

### San Diego Sunflower (*Bahiopsis laciniata*) — small shrub
Slope notes: *"Dry terraces, wall crowns, and **south-facing scrub banks**."* San Diego County–native bush daisy that fills sunny gaps between the larger shrubs.
**Also fine in:** Zones 1, 2.

### Golden Yarrow (*Eriophyllum confertiflorum*) — herbaceous perennial
"Dry slopes, rock gardens, and trail edges with sharp drainage." Bright yellow midsummer bloom complements the silvers and greens of the salvias and sagebrush.
**Also fine in:** Zones 1, 2.

## Zone 5 — Back: West-facing flat, dappled shade under tree (2–4 hrs filtered sun)

**Two plants.** A challenging zone for a chaparral-heavy inventory, but the tree-canopy + moister flat microclimate is actually a strong analog for the riparian and canyon-bottom habitats where a few specific keystones evolved.

### Blue Elderberry (*Sambucus mexicana*) — large shrub, keystone
Slope notes: *"**Moist canyon bottoms, north slopes**, or irrigated wildlife hedgerows."* Soil notes: *"Rich loam with steady moisture; tolerates clay if drainage is moderate."* This zone is the residential equivalent of its native habitat. The filtered light from above is ideal — full sun stresses elderberries inland, deep shade prevents fruiting, but dappled 2–4 hr sun yields generous bloom and berry crops. Supports 94 wildlife species; berries feed mockingbirds, finches, and waxwings.
**Also fine in:** Zone 3 (partial shade slope, if moister).

### Arroyo Willow (*Salix lasiolepis*) — large tree, keystone
"Seasonally moist swales, arroyo margins, and low spots." Although the documented sun requirement is "Full Sun," wild Arroyo Willows in coastal Southern California are often understory plants beneath cottonwoods and sycamores along creeks. The tree-canopy + flat-with-runoff-collection microclimate is a closer analog to its native habitat than an exposed dry flat would be. Caterpillar host to 118 wildlife species — willows are among the most ecologically productive trees on the continent.
**Also fine in:** Zone 2 (only with consistent supplemental irrigation).

## Planting strategy notes

- **Density:** Plant chaparral species *close* — they grow as interlocking communities in the wild, where shared canopy shade reduces evaporation and weed pressure. A south-facing slope of seven plants will fill in over 3–4 years.
- **Year-1 watering:** Even drought-tolerant natives need consistent water their first summer to develop deep roots. Taper aggressively from year 2 onward — overwatering kills more established natives than drought.
- **Mulch:** Use a coarse organic mulch (2–3" of shredded oak/cedar) on all zones *except* near the crowns of White Sage, Mission Manzanita, and Laurel Sumac, which rot if the crown stays wet.
- **Plant in fall:** October–December is the ideal planting window in Poway. Roots establish during the cool wet season; plants are ready for their first dry summer with a year of root growth behind them.
- **Zone 5 future expansion:** Consider adding shade-tolerant keystone species (*Frangula californica* / Coffeeberry; *Salvia spathacea* / Hummingbird Sage) to the inventory to round out Zone 5 with a proper oak-woodland understory layer.
